Gordon Ramsay

Gordon Ramsay is a British chef, restaurateur, television personality and writer. His restaurant group, Gordon Ramsay Restaurants, was founded in 1997 and has been awarded 17 Michelin stars overall; it currently holds a total of seven. His signature restaurant, Restaurant Gordon Ramsay in Chelsea, London, has held three Michelin stars since 2001. After rising to fame on the British television miniseries Boiling Point in 1999, Ramsay became one of the best-known and most influential chefs in the world.1

Ramsay's television appearances are defined by his bluntness, fiery temper, strict demeanour, and frequent use of profanity.He combines activities in the television, film, hospitality, and food industries, and has promoted and hired various chefs who have apprenticed under his wing. He is known for presenting television programmes about competitive cookery and food, such as the British series Hell's Kitchen (2004), Ramsay's Kitchen Nightmares (2004–2009, 2014), and The F Word (2005–2010), with Kitchen Nightmares winning the 2005 British Academy Television Award for Best Feature, and the American versions of Hell's Kitchen (2005–present), Kitchen Nightmares (2007–2014), MasterChef (2010–present), and MasterChef Junior (2013–present), as well as Hotel Hell (2012–2016), Gordon Behind Bars (2012), Gordon Ramsay's 24 Hours to Hell and Back (2018–2020), and Next Level Chef (2022–present).

Ramsay was appointed an OBE by Queen Elizabeth II in the 2006 New Year Honours list for services to the hospitality industry. He was named the top chef in the UK at the 2000 Catey Awards, and in July 2006 he won the Catey for Independent Restaurateur of the Year, becoming only the third person to have won three Catey Awards. In 2020, Forbes listed his earnings at US $70 million for the previous 12 months and ranked him at No. 19 on its list of the highest-earning celebrities.

Gordon Ramsay's Advice to Future Chefs

Gordon Ramsay advises young people between the ages of 16 and 30 to focus on acquiring knowledge rather than money in their early years.Don't take a job for the sake of money...go and get knowledge. He describes knowledge as the passport to better opportunities.He advises aspiring students to work for big chefs and move on when things become too comfortable.The money will come once you've mastered the craft.

“If you want to become a great chef, you have to work with great chefs. And that’s exactly what I did." Gordon Ramsay

Commonly Used Kitchen Jargon

Mise en place
Mise en place, French for "set in place," means that all the preparation work is neatly arranged and ready for cooking. This means that all the vegetables are peeled and chopped as needed, the meat is thawed and ready to cook, and everything is in separate bowls or containers.
Front of house
This refers to the floor or dining area where managers, waiters, and bartenders interact with customers. It can also refer to the serving or hosting staff as well.
Back of house
This refers to the kitchen or the storage rooms. Back of the house may also refer to the people that run it: chefs, cooks, and dishwashers.
Expedite
To coordinate order tickets from the front of house staff to the chefs and cooks in the kitchen. The expediter also assembles the orders that are ready for pick-up and makes sure that every plate is perfect.
In the weeds
This expression means that the server (usually the FOH staff) is overloaded with work. When someone says "in the weeds," it usually means they need help serving a large group of people in the dining area.
Deuce
This expression simply means a table for two.
Reach-in
A reach-in is an upright refrigerator that is typically used to hold prepped ingredients or ingredients that cannot be kept at room temperature for long periods of time.
